# Effective Study Techniques

A practical guide to getting more from your learning time

> Welcome. This presentation covers five evidence-based techniques that help you retain information more effectively and make better use of limited study time. Each one is grounded in decades of cognitive science research.

## Active Recall

- Close your notes and retrieve the material from memory.
- Testing yourself is far more effective than re-reading or highlighting.
- Flashcards, practice questions, and blank-page summaries all work well.
- The harder the retrieval, the stronger the memory trace.

**Example:** Topic is "causes of World War I", close your notes and write freely. You recall nationalism, alliances, and assassination but miss the economic pressures. That gap tells you exactly what to re-read next.

> Active recall forces the brain to reconstruct information rather than passively recognise it. The effort of retrieval is what strengthens long-term retention. This is sometimes called the testing effect.

---

## Spaced Repetition

- Review material at increasing intervals over time.
- Revisit after one day, then three days, then a week, then a month.
- Each successful retrieval resets and extends the next review interval.
- Apps like Anki automate the scheduling for you.

**Example:** 50 vocabulary cards take 30 minutes on day one. By day 5, only the difficult cards remain, and by day 30 the session takes under 2 minutes. Total time invested is less than a single cramming session, with far better retention.

> Cramming produces short-term recall but poor retention. Spreading reviews over time makes each session progressively shorter as the material becomes more stable. The key insight is that forgetting a little before each review is intentional.

## The Feynman Technique

1. Choose a concept and write it down as a topic heading.
2. Explain it in plain language as if teaching someone unfamiliar with it.
3. Identify gaps where your explanation breaks down or becomes vague.
4. Return to the source material and fill exactly those gaps.

**Example:** Topic is "supply and demand". Attempt: "when price rises, fewer people buy", but you cannot explain why the curve shifts. That gap sends you back to one specific section, not the whole chapter.

> Named after physicist Richard Feynman. The point is not to simplify for others but to expose your own blind spots. If you cannot explain something simply, you do not yet fully understand it.

## Audio Learning

- Convert notes and slide decks to audio files using text-to-speech tools.
- Listen during commutes, exercise, cooking, or other downtime.
- Extends available study time without adding screen time or effort.
- Works especially well for review, not first exposure to new material.

**Example:** A 25-minute commute plus a 15-minute lunch walk is 40 minutes of review per day. Over one semester that adds up to over 60 hours of additional study time. No extra desk time or screen time required.

> The constraint on study time is usually not motivation but available hours. Audio learning removes that constraint by using time that would otherwise be unavailable. Tools like SlideNotesTTS make the conversion straightforward.

## Combining Methods

**Active recall** surfaces what you do not yet know.

**Spaced repetition** locks in what you have already learned.

**Feynman** deepens understanding of weak and unclear areas.

**Audio** extends the hours available for review each day.

**Example:** Start each day with a short audio review during your commute. Follow up with 10 minutes of active recall at lunch. End the day with Anki: the session is short because spaced repetition does the work.

> No technique works in isolation. Used together, they cover the full cycle from initial learning through consolidation to fluent recall. Start with one and add the others gradually.

---

## Summary

- **Retrieve, not re-read:** active recall beats passive review every time.
- **Spread it out:** short, frequent sessions beat marathon cramming.
- **Explain it simply:** if you cannot, find the gap and fix it.
- **Use dead time:** audio turns commutes into study sessions.

**Example:** Week before an exam, 30 minutes of active recall each day. Each session surfaces gaps. Fill them with targeted reading only. By exam day, each topic has been reviewed multiple times with minimal total effort.

> The research on all of these techniques is consistent and goes back decades. The difficulty is habit, not knowledge. Pick one technique, apply it for two weeks, then layer in the next.
